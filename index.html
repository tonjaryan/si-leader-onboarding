Section 1 — Understanding SI

What is Supplemental Instruction?

Build your foundation before training day

SI is a peer-assisted academic support program that targets historically difficult courses — not struggling students. As an SI Leader, you attended or are currently enrolled in the same course, so you understand the material and the challenges firsthand.

  • SI sessions are collaborative — you guide students to learn from each other, not lecture at them.
  • SI is proactive — all students are welcome, not just those falling behind.
  • Unlike tutoring, SI Leaders are facilitators, not answer-givers.
  • Being an SI Leader develops your own leadership, communication, and mastery of the subject.

Section 1 — The SI Philosophy

SI is not passive learning

Understanding what makes SI fundamentally different

Many students arrive at SI expecting a tutor to walk them through answers. A large part of your role is actively resisting that dynamic. The goal is for students to discover, reason through, and confirm knowledge on their own and with each other — not to receive it from you.

Passive learning (what SI is not)
  • Students sit and listen while a leader explains content
  • The leader is the authority — students wait to be told
  • Learning depends on the quality of the person at the front
  • Students leave relying on someone outside themselves
SI (active, peer-driven learning)
  • Students work through problems collaboratively — you facilitate
  • The group is the resource — peers teach each other
  • Learning depends on the quality of the group's thinking
  • Students leave trusting their own ability to find answers
The outcome that matters

Students who regularly attend SI sessions average one full letter grade higher than those who do not — not because the SI leader taught them more, but because they learned how to learn with each other.

What this means for you as an SI leader
  • Your job is to design situations where students must think, not situations where you explain
  • Resist the urge to confirm answers immediately — let the group wrestle with uncertainty first
  • When a student says "I don't know," your response is "Who in the room has a thought on that?" — not the answer
  • The measure of a great SI session is how much the students talked, not how much you did

Section 2 — Core Facilitation

The 3 Core Facilitation Techniques

Select each technique to expand details

01 — Wait Time (Think-Pair-Share)

▼
What it is

After asking a question, pause deliberately for 5–10 seconds before calling on anyone. In Think-Pair-Share, students think alone, then discuss with a partner, then share with the group.

Why it matters

It equalizes participation, gives introverts time to formulate answers, and dramatically improves response quality. Most instructors wait less than 1 second — a brief pause changes everything.

Common challenge

The silence feels uncomfortable. You'll want to fill it. Resist the urge.

When to pivot

If no one responds after a full wait, rephrase the question or offer a scaffold, then redirect again.

02 — Redirecting Questions

▼
What it is

When a student asks you a question, bounce it back to the group: "That's a great question — what does everyone else think?" or "Who can help answer that?"

Why it matters

It keeps the session student-centered. Students learn more when they arrive at answers themselves. It builds group cohesion and reduces dependence on the SI Leader as the answer source.

Common challenge

Students expect you to just tell them. Some may be frustrated at first — that's okay. Stay consistent.

When to pivot

If the question involves a factual error or logistics that genuinely require a direct answer from you, give it concisely, then return to facilitation mode.

03 — Check for Understanding (Clusters)

▼
What it is

Regularly verify that the group truly understands before moving on. Use targeted questions, quick polls, clusters (small-group checks), or exit tickets.

Why it matters

Students often think they understand until asked to explain or apply a concept. Real-time checks reveal hidden gaps and let you adjust the session on the fly.

Common challenge

Students nod and say "yes" even when confused. Use open-ended checks — "Can you explain that in your own words?" — not yes/no questions.

When to pivot

If time is short, condense checks to one targeted question rather than skipping them entirely.

Section 4 — Connecting with Your Professor

Write your professor introduction email

Your professor is your partner in SI — introduce yourself before your first session.

What to include
  • Your name, major, and year
  • That you are the SI leader for their course
  • Your commitment to attend every class
  • Your two SI session times and locations
  • An open invitation to collaborate as much or as little as they like
Tone and expectations
  • Keep it brief and professional — 3 short paragraphs is ideal
  • Some professors will share exam topics or practice problems with you — welcome that
  • Others prefer no involvement — that is equally fine, do not pressure them
  • End with your contact info and an open door to reach you

"Dear Professor [Last Name], my name is [Your Name] and I am a [year, major] student at UT Austin. I have been selected as the Supplemental Instruction leader for your [Course Name] course this semester through the Sanger Learning Center. I wanted to reach out to introduce myself and let you know that I will be attending every class session so I can stay aligned with your instruction and best support your students..."

Section 5 — Example Lesson Plan

Lesson plan 1 — Sequencing for the MCAT

50-minute session · Group-based · MCAT prep

Lesson objective

Students will be able to identify and apply sequential ordering strategies to MCAT-style passages by physically manipulating story fragments, collaboratively reconstructing narrative logic, and transferring that reasoning process to an authentic MCAT excerpt.

Lesson plan
How does this support SI?
5min
Icebreaker
Non-academic quick check-in
SI leader poses one low-stakes personal question — e.g. "What is one thing you watched, read, or listened to this week that you'd recommend?" Students share briefly (20–30 sec each). SI leader participates too, modeling peer-level engagement. No right or wrong answer — reduces anxiety before academic content begins.
Your reflection
20min
Group activity
Cut-up story sequencing
SI leader pre-cuts 3–4 short narratives into individual sentence strips — all shuffled together in an envelope per group.

Part A (8 min) — Sort by story: Small groups (3–4 students) sort strips by which story each belongs to, justifying choices aloud.

Part B (7 min) — Sequence within stories: Groups arrange each story's strips in correct order. Every member must be able to explain the final sequence. SI leader circulates with probing questions — does not give answers.
Your reflection
12min
Whole-group debrief
Sequencing strategies + MCAT connection
SI leader brings the full group back. Discussion is facilitated — not lectured. Key questions: "What cues did you use to decide which strip came first?" (hold 5-sec wait time) · "Did your group disagree? How did you resolve it?" · SI leader redirects answers back to group rather than commenting. Bridge: "Where in the MCAT might you need to sequence information?" Records 2–3 student-generated strategies on the board.
Your reflection
13min
Application
MCAT excerpt — paired sequencing practice
SI leader distributes a real MCAT-style passage requiring sequential reasoning. Students work in pairs to annotate together — identifying sequence shifts, cause-effect, and chronological signals. Each pair answers 1–2 targeted questions, then shares one insight with the group. SI leader checks for understanding: "Can someone restate what that pair found in different words?" Wrap-up: each student writes one sentence connecting today's activity to a study strategy they will use this week.
